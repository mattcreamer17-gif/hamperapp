Layer above the app where a lot of the pain lives

The 60 / 30 / 10 on an on-demand laundry network

We work one layer above the app. Most problems inside a multi-unit operator are not AI problems.

60%

Standard code and data work

Ticketing, labeling, dispatch. The Postgres layer. Cheapest to build, cheapest to run, hardest to get wrong once.

30%

Rule-based logic

"If the bag is flagged delicate and the partner is Shop B, route differently." Declarative rules you can read without a PhD.

10%

Real AI questions

Classifying fuzzy support tickets. Flagging anomalous orders. Predicting which partner is about to have a bad week.

We help operators decide which layer each problem belongs on before anyone writes code. Software gets cheaper. Wrong scope gets expensive.

Three bets we would test with you in thirty minutes

  1. Bet 01

    Partner quality variance is the ceiling on growth.

    Sixty partner shops, each with their own SOPs, means every new hotel or STR contract inherits whichever operator runs the route that week. A CEO at Hamperapp would confirm in thirty seconds that the biggest open issue in ops reviews is "partner X had a bad week."

  2. Bet 02

    Customer-service triage is a human bottleneck.

    Review patterns show miscommunication, then a supervisor making it right, over and over. There is no structured playbook for the top three failure modes (lost item, wrong label, fee dispute). A routing layer that closes the first seventy percent of tickets on a standard resolution reclaims a meaningful fraction of the support team's week.

  3. Bet 03

    The hospitality pivot needs a productized operator onboarding flow.

    Hotels and STR operators buy on SLAs. The current partner network was built for consumer pickup. Onboarding a new commercial partner into a repeatable service tier is the wedge that turns Hamperapp from a consumer marketplace into a B2B platform.

What the thirty minutes looks like

Five questions. Your answers tell us whether there is a real engagement or whether we should shake hands and move on.

  • 01. When a bag comes back under the wrong name or short an item, who owns the ticket from open to close, and how many hands does it pass through?
  • 02. Across your 60 partner laundromats, how much of the service standard lives in a document versus in someone's head at each shop?
  • 03. On the hospitality side, what does a typical new hotel or STR onboarding look like right now, and where does it stall?
  • 04. When a partner has a bad week, what is the first signal you see, and how long after the customer feels it?
  • 05. If you could point a small engagement at one operator workflow this quarter, which one would pay back fastest?
